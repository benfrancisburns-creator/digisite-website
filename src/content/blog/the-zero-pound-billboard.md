Let's do some quick maths.

You're a scaffold company. You've got a job on a city-centre high street — a residential conversion, 14 months on site. Your scaffold goes up on the front elevation, facing the street. Standard job. Standard price.

That scaffold face is approximately 12 metres wide and 8 metres tall. It's at eye level for pedestrians and drivers. It's on a street with 40,000+ daily footfall. It'll be there for over a year.

In the advertising industry, that's called a **96-sheet equivalent digital billboard in a premium urban location**. A comparable screen in that position would sell for **£3,000-£8,000 per month** in advertising revenue.

Over 14 months, that's somewhere between **£42,000 and £112,000** in potential revenue.

Your scaffold is currently generating **£0** of that.

## Where the Money Goes Today

Right now, the advertising money that *should* be flowing through construction sites goes to:

- **JCDecaux, Clear Channel, and Global** — who control most permanent DOOH screens
- **Shopping centre landlords** — who charge for screens in malls and retail parks
- **Transport operators** — who sell screen space at stations and on buses
- **Big tech** — where an increasing share of advertising spend goes to Google, Meta, and TikTok

None of these channels are paying scaffold companies. None of them are sharing revenue with the construction industry. And yet the construction industry is providing some of the most visible, valuable real estate in every city — for free.

## The Revenue Share Model

Here's how it works when construction sites are brought into the advertising ecosystem:

1. **A digital screen is installed** on your scaffold or perimeter hoarding — at no cost to you
2. **Advertising is sold** programmatically through existing DOOH platforms
3. **Revenue is shared** between the screen operator and the construction partner
4. **When the scaffold comes down**, the screen is removed and redeployed to the next site

The construction company doesn't buy anything. Doesn't sell anything. Doesn't manage anything. They simply allow a screen to be attached to a structure they were building anyway — and receive a share of the revenue it generates.

## "But My Clients Won't Want Advertising on Their Building"

This is the most common pushback. And it's worth addressing head-on.

First: the developer or client **also receives value**. In many models, they receive a share of advertising revenue too — turning a cost line (hoarding maintenance) into an income line.

Second: the screen can display **the developer's own branding and marketing** alongside third-party advertising. Selling apartments? Show the development CGIs and pricing on the screen. Promoting the scheme to passers-by? It's a better advertisement for the project than a faded vinyl banner could ever be.

Third: the quality of the installation is dramatically higher than a traditional wrap. A well-lit, well-maintained digital screen improves the look of a construction site — it doesn't detract from it.

And fourth: the alternative is what's there now. Bare scaffold. Torn netting. Dead hoardings. Nobody's client is proud of that.

## What About Planning Permission?

This is a legitimate question, and the answer depends on the specific location and local authority. In the UK, advertising on construction sites typically falls under the **Town and Country Planning (Control of Advertisements) Regulations 2007**. Temporary advertisements on construction hoardings often benefit from deemed consent — though digital displays may require express consent.

The key point: **this is a solvable problem, not a blocker**. Companies already navigate advertising consent for bus shelters, phone boxes, and roadside billboards. The same compliance frameworks apply to construction-site media. It requires expertise, but it's not uncharted territory.

## The Numbers That Matter

For a scaffold company running 20 active urban sites at any given time:

- Average screen revenue per site: **£4,000/month** (conservative)
- Partner revenue share at 30%: **£1,200/month per site**
- Across 20 sites: **£24,000/month**
- Annual passive income: **£288,000**

That's nearly **£300,000 per year** in revenue from structures you were building anyway. No additional labour. No additional materials. No additional risk.

For larger operators with 50+ urban sites, the numbers cross into seven figures.

## Who Moves First Wins

The scaffold advertising market doesn't exist yet in the UK — at least not at scale. That means the first scaffold companies to partner with a digital advertising platform will get the best terms, the most prominent placements, and the strongest relationships.

This is a land grab. The inventory is the scaffolding. And the companies who own that inventory are sitting on a goldmine they don't even know about.

The question is simple: **how long are you going to keep giving it away for free?**
