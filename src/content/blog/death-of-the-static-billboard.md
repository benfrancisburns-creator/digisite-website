There's a billboard on the M62 near Warrington. It's advertising a car that was discontinued two years ago. The colours have faded. One corner of the vinyl has peeled away. It's been raining on it since it went up, and it shows.

Nobody at the car brand knows it's still there. Nobody at the media owner has been asked to replace it. It just... exists. A ghost ad for a ghost product, seen by 90,000 drivers a day who register absolutely nothing.

This is the static billboard industry in 2026. And it's dying.

## The Numbers Don't Lie

In 2015, static formats accounted for **78%** of UK out-of-home advertising revenue. By 2025, that had flipped — digital now commands over **66%** of OOH spend, and the gap widens every quarter.

The trajectory is clear. Within five years, static will be a niche format — used only where digital isn't feasible or where the cost can't be justified. The premium end of the market has already moved entirely to digital.

Why? Because static billboards can't do any of the things modern advertisers need:

- **They can't update in real time.** A static campaign takes days to print, transport, and paste. A digital campaign goes live in seconds.
- **They can't daypart.** A static billboard shows the same message at 3am as it does at 3pm. Digital screens can run breakfast campaigns in the morning, lunch offers at noon, and entertainment ads in the evening.
- **They can't be programmatic.** Static can't connect to demand-side platforms. Digital can — meaning any screen can be bought programmatically by any advertiser, in real time, with no human intervention.
- **They can't measure.** A static billboard has no idea how many people saw it. Digital screens integrate with footfall counters, traffic data, and mobile analytics to report verified impressions.
- **They can't serve multiple clients.** One static billboard = one advertiser. One digital screen can serve dozens of campaigns per day, maximising revenue per square metre.

## What Killed Static

It wasn't one thing. It was everything at once.

**Programmatic DOOH** made it possible for brands to buy outdoor advertising the same way they buy online ads — in real time, targeted by location, audience, and context. Once advertisers tasted that level of control, going back to a six-week static booking felt prehistoric.

**Falling hardware costs** made digital screens economically viable in locations where they previously weren't. Five years ago, only the highest-traffic sites could justify a digital screen. Today, the economics work for a much wider range of locations.

**Data expectations** changed what advertisers demand. Every other channel — social, search, display, TV — provides detailed performance metrics. Static OOH is the only channel where the reporting is basically "trust us, people probably saw it." That's no longer acceptable.

**Sustainability pressure** played a role too. Static billboards generate enormous amounts of waste — printed vinyl that's used once and sent to landfill. Digital screens eliminate that waste entirely.

## The Static Holdouts

Static won't disappear completely. There are locations where it still makes sense:

- **Rural roads** where traffic doesn't justify digital infrastructure
- **Fly-posting** and grassroots marketing where lo-fi is the point
- **Artistic and community murals** where the medium is the message

But in cities? On high streets? Along arterial roads? Static is finished. The advertisers have moved on. The technology has moved on. The only thing holding static in place is inertia — and the fact that someone, somewhere, is still paying the lease.

## The Next Frontier

Here's the interesting part. The traditional DOOH industry has done an excellent job of converting existing static locations to digital — bus shelters, roadside 6-sheets, motorway gantries, shopping centre panels. That conversion is largely complete.

So where does the next wave of digital OOH inventory come from?

The answer is **new locations that never had advertising before**. And the biggest untapped category is construction infrastructure.

Think about it. Construction scaffolding and perimeter hoardings are:

- In **prime urban locations** (city centres, high streets, transport hubs)
- Present for **months at a time** (6-18 month average)
- **Large format** (comparable to 48-sheet and 96-sheet billboards)
- **Already being built** (no additional infrastructure needed)

The static billboard is dying because it can't keep up with what advertisers need. But the *locations* that made billboards valuable haven't gone anywhere. What's changed is the technology on them — and the places that technology can now reach.

Construction sites are the obvious next step. The infrastructure is already there. It's just waiting to be switched on.

## The Irony

The final irony is this: the construction industry builds the cities where billboards live. Construction companies erect the structures, pour the foundations, and wire the electricity that makes DOOH possible.

And yet the construction industry itself captures **zero** of the advertising revenue that its work creates. The scaffolding that sits in a premium location for a year? Dead space. The hoarding that wraps a city-centre development? A cost, not an asset.

The static billboard is dead. But the opportunity it leaves behind is enormous — and it's the construction industry, of all industries, that's best placed to fill it.
