There's a paradox at the heart of urban advertising.

The locations where people complain the most — the construction sites, the hoardings, the scaffolded buildings — are also the locations with the highest commercial value. And nobody's monetising them.

Think about where construction happens. It doesn't happen in quiet suburbs or industrial estates (well, it does, but that's not the interesting part). The big, visible, disruptive construction projects happen in **city centres, high streets, transport hubs, and regeneration zones**. The places with the most footfall. The most traffic. The most eyeballs.

In other words: the places where advertisers pay the most to be seen.

## The Ugly Premium

In outdoor advertising, there's a well-understood hierarchy of location value:

1. **Transport hubs** — Stations, airports, bus terminals. Captive audiences, high dwell time.
2. **High streets** — Retail footfall, daytime visibility, proximity to spending.
3. **Arterial roads** — Vehicle traffic, commuter routes, repetitive exposure.
4. **Residential areas** — Lower traffic, but hyper-local targeting potential.

Now look at where major construction projects happen. City-centre residential towers. High-street retail conversions. Station-adjacent developments. Arterial road infrastructure.

Construction sites cluster in **the most valuable advertising locations** because those are the places where development makes economic sense. Developers build where land values are high, which is where footfall is high, which is where advertisers want to be.

The ugliest corners of your city — the ones wrapped in scaffold and surrounded by hoardings — are sitting on some of the most valuable advertising real estate in the country. They just don't know it.

## Look Around You — Advertisers Are Already Paying

Walk down any high street in a UK city and count the screens. Digital bus shelters. Roadside 6-sheets. Large-format billboards on building gables. Screens inside shopping centres, at train stations, on the sides of phone boxes. Every single one of them is generating revenue — because advertisers are willing to pay good money to be seen in high-traffic urban locations.

And they're paying well. The UK out-of-home advertising market is worth over **£1.4 billion**, growing at more than 10% a year, with digital formats commanding the majority of spend. Demand for premium urban screen space has never been higher. Media owners can't build screens fast enough.

That's the context. Now look at the scaffold wrapped around the building on that same high street. It's in the same location. Facing the same audience. Often bigger and more prominent than the screens around it. It's been there for months — and it'll be there for months more.

The advertisers walking past it every day are spending thousands on the bus shelter across the road. They'd spend on the scaffold too — **if it had a screen**.

This isn't a criticism of the OOH industry. It's a compliment. They've proven that urban screen space is valuable, that advertisers will pay for it, and that the demand is growing. All DigiSite is doing is opening up a new category of that same inventory — one that's been hiding in plain sight on every construction site in the country.

## The Attention Economy

Advertising is fundamentally about attention. And here's the counterintuitive truth about construction sites: **people look at them**.

You might think people ignore scaffolding. But research on visual attention in urban environments tells a different story. Construction activity draws the eye. Scaffolding changes the visual landscape of a street. Hoardings block the normal sightlines and force pedestrians to engage with whatever's in front of them.

Drivers slow down near construction sites. Pedestrians are funnelled onto narrower pavements, increasing proximity to the site boundary. And the sheer novelty of a changing streetscape — something new where something familiar used to be — captures attention in a way that a permanent billboard simply can't.

Construction sites are **attention magnets**. They're just not capitalising on it.

## The Valuation Gap

Let's put some numbers on it.

A DigiScaff screen on a city-centre scaffold face runs six advertiser slots in a rotating loop. Each slot sells at approximately **£2,500 per fortnight** — the industry-standard booking cycle for UK out-of-home advertising. That's **£15,000 per fortnight** at full utilisation, or roughly **£32,500 per month** in gross advertising revenue.

Over a 12-month scaffold programme, that single screen generates approximately **£390,000** in gross revenue.

The scaffold company's hire income from that same job? Typically **£2,000-£4,000 per month**.

The advertising value of the scaffold face is **eight to sixteen times the hire value of the scaffold itself**. And right now, 100% of that advertising value is being left on the table.

Add a run of DigiPerim screens along the perimeter hoarding — five panels across a 15-metre frontage, each with six slots at £1,000 per slot per fortnight — and the combined site generates over **£1.17 million gross per year**.

## Why Nobody's Done This Before

The honest answer: because it falls between two industries that don't talk to each other.

The **construction industry** knows how to build things. It doesn't think about media, advertising, or digital content. Scaffold companies price jobs based on tonnage, duration, and complexity — not on footfall data or CPM rates.

The **advertising industry** knows how to buy and sell attention. But it's built around permanent infrastructure — fixed billboard sites, street furniture contracts, transport network deals. It doesn't have relationships with scaffold companies, and it doesn't understand construction programmes.

The gap between these two industries is where the opportunity lives. And it's enormous.

## Reframing Ugly

Here's the shift that needs to happen: stop seeing construction sites as disruption, and start seeing them as **temporary infrastructure in premium locations**.

A scaffold isn't ugly — it's a large-format media structure in a high-traffic position. A hoarding isn't an eyesore — it's a blank canvas at pedestrian eye level. A construction site isn't a nuisance — it's a pop-up venue for digital advertising that appears in locations where permanent media can't.

The ugliest corners of your city are worth millions. Someone just needs to switch on the screens.
