There's a moment, somewhere around 2019, when cities stopped being cities and started being media platforms.

It happened gradually, then all at once. Bus shelters became digital. Phone boxes became ad panels. Taxi roofs got screens. Lift doors, petrol pumps, supermarket freezer cabinets, gym mirrors — all screens now. The average Londoner sees somewhere between 3,000 and 5,000 advertising messages a day. Most of them on surfaces that didn't carry ads ten years ago.

The instinctive reaction is to recoil. More ads? More noise? More visual clutter in an already overwhelming world?

But here's the thing nobody talks about: most of it has made cities *better*.

## The Trade Nobody Notices

Every digital bus shelter in the UK exists because of a deal. A media company — usually JCDecaux or Clear Channel — builds, installs, and maintains the shelter at no cost to the council. In return, they sell advertising on the panels. The council gets free street furniture. The public gets a roof when it rains. The advertiser gets eyeballs. Everyone wins.

This model — infrastructure funded by advertising — is older than the internet. It's how commercial television works. It's how Google works. It's how most of the free services we use every day work. But when it shows up in physical space, people get uneasy.

The irony is that ad-funded infrastructure often looks *better* than the publicly funded version it replaced. Compare a modern digital bus shelter — clean glass, LED lighting, real-time bus information — with the council-maintained shelters of the 1990s. Cracked perspex. Graffiti. Missing panels. No lighting. The ad-funded version is objectively a better piece of street furniture.

## When Screens Replace Eyesores

This pattern repeats everywhere you look.

The old BT phone boxes that were rusting on every high street? Replaced by sleek digital totems that provide free Wi-Fi, USB charging, and wayfinding maps. Funded by the screens on each side.

Railway station concourses that used to be lined with peeling paper posters? Now bright, well-lit spaces with digital directories that actually help you find your platform. Funded by the brands running campaigns between the wayfinding content.

Shopping centre car parks that used to feel unsafe after dark? Now well-lit, with digital signage at every level. You guessed it — ad-funded.

The pattern is consistent: when advertising revenue flows into a space, the quality of that space goes up. Not because advertisers are philanthropists, but because they need their screens to look good. A premium brand won't run a campaign on a dirty, broken panel next to a bin. So the infrastructure around the screen gets maintained, lit, cleaned, and upgraded — all on the media owner's budget.

## The Attention Contract

There's an implicit contract in all of this, and it's worth making explicit.

When you glance at a digital screen on a bus shelter, you're not being exploited. You're participating in a transaction. Your momentary attention — a fraction of a second, usually — funds the shelter you're standing under. It funds the Wi-Fi totem you connected to. It funds the real-time departure board you just checked.

This isn't a new idea. It's how commercial media has always worked. The difference is that it's now happening in physical space, on physical surfaces, in the cities where we live and work.

And the exchange rate is remarkably good. A single glance at a DOOH screen is worth a fraction of a penny to the advertiser. Multiply that by thousands of people per day, and it funds genuine public infrastructure. Your attention — given freely, for less than a second — pays for things that would otherwise come out of council budgets that don't exist.

## The Line That Matters

None of this means every surface *should* be a screen. There's a line, and it matters.

Screens in transport hubs, retail environments, and commercial districts? That's where people expect commercial messaging. It's appropriate, and when done well, it genuinely improves the built environment.

Screens in residential streets, parks, conservation areas, and places of worship? That's where the line should hold. Not every space is a commercial space, and the advertising industry — to its credit — largely respects this distinction.

The planning system exists precisely to enforce this boundary. Advertisement consent regulations in the UK are among the most robust in the world. Every new advertising display in a public space requires approval. The system isn't perfect, but it works — and it's the reason British cities haven't turned into Times Square.

## What Comes Next

The UK's out-of-home advertising market is growing at over 10% year-on-year. Digital formats now account for the majority of spend. And the industry is running out of traditional locations to digitise.

That means the next wave of DOOH growth will come from surfaces and locations that have never carried advertising before. Some of those will be controversial. Some will be brilliant. Some will be both.

The question isn't whether more urban surfaces will become screens — they will. The question is whether we're thoughtful about which surfaces, in which locations, governed by what rules.

Get that right, and the cities of 2030 will be better-lit, better-maintained, and better-funded than the ones we have today. All paid for by the fraction of a second you spend glancing at a screen you barely notice.
