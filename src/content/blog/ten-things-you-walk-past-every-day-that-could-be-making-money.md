Every city is full of dead space. Infrastructure that exists purely for function — holding things up, keeping things in, getting people from A to B. Nobody looks at it. Nobody thinks about it. And nobody's making money from it.

But what if they could?

Here are 10 pieces of everyday urban infrastructure that are sitting on untapped commercial potential — and some are closer to being monetised than you think.

## 1. Construction Scaffolding

**Where you see it:** Every high street, every city centre, every time a building goes up, comes down, or gets a facelift.

**Why it's valuable:** Scaffolding occupies prime locations for months at a time. A single scaffold face on a busy street can generate 30,000-50,000 daily impressions. At standard DOOH rates, that's worth thousands per month.

**Status:** This is the one we're doing something about. Digital screens on scaffold faces and perimeter hoardings are about to become a thing. The technology exists, the advertising demand exists, and the infrastructure is literally being built every day.

## 2. Perimeter Hoardings

**Where you see it:** Around every construction site, development plot, and demolition zone.

**Why it's valuable:** Hoardings are long, flat, at eye level, and face the street. They're essentially ready-made billboard positions. Yet almost all of them display nothing more useful than a developer's logo and a planning reference number.

**Status:** Starting to change. Smart hoardings with integrated digital panels are being trialled in several UK cities. The challenge is standardisation — every site is different.

## 3. Multi-Storey Car Parks

**Where you see it:** In every town and city centre. Usually the ugliest buildings on the block.

**Why it's valuable:** Car parks have enormous flat exterior walls facing busy roads. Drivers approach them slowly (looking for the entrance), creating high dwell time. And they're often in central locations near retail and entertainment.

**Status:** A few operators have started wrapping car park exteriors with digital screens, particularly NCP and Q-Park in larger cities. But the vast majority remain untapped.

## 4. Railway Bridges

**Where you see it:** Every city has them — bridges carrying railway lines over roads, often on busy commuter routes.

**Why it's valuable:** Railway bridges are at driver and pedestrian eye level, they span the full width of a road, and traffic stops or slows underneath them. The audience is captive.

**Status:** Network Rail has explored advertising on some bridge structures, but regulatory and heritage concerns limit progress. This one's further out.

## 5. Utility Cabinets

**Where you see it:** Those grey or green metal boxes on every pavement — telecom, electricity, broadband distribution.

**Why it's valuable:** There are **hundreds of thousands** of them across the UK. Individually they're small, but collectively they represent a vast network of touchpoints at street level. Some companies are already wrapping them with vinyl ads.

**Status:** BT has monetised some of its street cabinets, and startups like Digibox have explored digital screens on utility furniture. Scale is the challenge — each unit is small, so revenue per location is limited.

## 6. Bin Lorries

**Where you see it:** Every residential and commercial street, multiple times per week.

**Why it's valuable:** Bin lorries move slowly through residential areas during daytime hours. They stop frequently. They're large, visible vehicles in locations that other advertising can't easily reach. Some councils already sell ad space on them.

**Status:** Council fleet advertising exists but is mostly static vinyl. Digital screens on waste collection vehicles would be an interesting evolution — though the economics are tricky given the vehicle cost and maintenance environment.

## 7. Canal Bridges and Towpaths

**Where you see it:** Cities like Birmingham, Manchester, Leeds, and London have extensive urban canal networks, increasingly used as pedestrian and cycling routes.

**Why it's valuable:** Canal towpaths are becoming prime commuter and leisure routes. The audience is growing. Bridge abutments and canalside walls offer flat, visible surfaces in otherwise media-dark environments.

**Status:** Almost entirely untapped. A few canal-side developments have incorporated screens, but nobody's systematically approached canal infrastructure as an advertising medium.

## 8. Temporary Event Fencing

**Where you see it:** Around festivals, concerts, sporting events, markets, and any temporary public event.

**Why it's valuable:** Event audiences are highly targeted, highly engaged, and in a spending mindset. Fencing goes up for days or weeks and is seen by thousands. It's essentially the same proposition as construction hoardings, but with a defined, premium audience.

**Status:** Some event organisers sell static advertising on hoardings. Digital event fencing is emerging but not yet widespread in the UK.

## 9. Empty Shop Windows

**Where you see it:** Every high street in the country. The UK has a retail vacancy rate of around **14%**, meaning one in seven shops is sitting empty.

**Why it's valuable:** Empty shops are in retail locations — by definition, that means high footfall. The windows are large, clean, and at perfect viewing height. A digital screen in a vacant shop window would generate impressions in a location that's currently generating nothing.

**Status:** A few prop-tech startups have tried to place screens in vacant units, but landlord cooperation and power supply are persistent barriers. The concept is sound; the execution is complex.

## 10. Substation Walls

**Where you see it:** Electrical substations are everywhere in urban areas — small brick or metal buildings, usually surrounded by security fencing. Often located on main roads.

**Why it's valuable:** Substations have secure power supply (obviously), flat exterior walls, and are often in commercially valuable locations. They're maintained by utility companies who could welcome a revenue-sharing arrangement.

**Status:** Barely explored. Utility companies are conservative, and substations raise safety concerns around public interaction. But the physical infrastructure is ideal for small-format digital screens.

## The Common Thread

All ten of these share the same characteristics:

- They're **already built** — no new infrastructure needed
- They're in **high-traffic locations** — where people already are
- They generate **zero revenue** today — pure dead space
- They could support **digital advertising** with relatively simple modifications

The out-of-home advertising industry has spent decades fighting for the same limited pool of billboard sites, bus shelters, and station platforms. Meanwhile, cities are full of overlooked infrastructure that could carry media — if someone builds the operational model to make it work.

Construction scaffolding and hoardings are the most obvious starting point. They're large, prominent, long-duration, and in prime locations. They're the low-hanging fruit of urban dead space.

And they're about to start earning their keep.
