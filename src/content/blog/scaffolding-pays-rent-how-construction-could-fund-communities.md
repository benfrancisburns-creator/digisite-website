Nobody likes living next to a building site.

The noise. The dust. The narrowed pavements. The lorries blocking your street at 7am. For months — sometimes years — local residents and businesses put up with significant disruption while someone else's project gets built.

And what do they get in return? Nothing. Maybe a leaflet through the door with a helpline number.

But what if the construction site was actively giving back?

## The Idea

Here's a simple proposition: **construction-site advertising generates revenue. A portion of that revenue goes back into the community where the construction is happening.**

Not as charity. Not as a PR exercise. As a structured, transparent model that's baked into the way construction-site media works.

A scaffold face on a busy street generates advertising revenue — potentially thousands of pounds per month. If a percentage of that revenue was ring-fenced for community benefit, the impact over a 12-18 month build programme would be meaningful:

- **£500/month** could fund a local sports club, youth group, or community garden
- **£1,000/month** could support a neighbourhood clean-up programme or street art project
- **£2,000/month** could part-fund a community liaison role, giving residents a direct voice in how the construction site is managed

These aren't enormous sums individually. But across dozens of sites in a city, they add up to something significant.

## Why This Makes Business Sense

Cynics will say this is just marketing. And yes, there's a commercial benefit — developers and contractors who invest in community relations face fewer objections, fewer delays, and smoother planning processes.

But the business case goes deeper than reputation management:

**Planning applications move faster** when communities feel heard and valued. A single objection from a residents' group can delay a project by months. The cost of that delay dwarfs a few thousand pounds in community investment.

**Construction sites with good community relations experience less vandalism and theft.** The British Security Industry Association estimates construction site crime costs the UK industry over **£800 million per year**. A site that the local community feels ownership over is a site that's better protected.

**Local authorities are increasingly requiring developers to demonstrate community benefit.** Section 106 agreements, Community Infrastructure Levies, and social value assessments are standard in the planning process. Advertising-funded community investment is a tangible, measurable way to deliver social value.

## What Community Funding Could Look Like

Imagine a digital screen on a construction hoarding in a residential area. The screen runs commercial advertising during peak hours — morning commute, lunchtime, evening. But it also:

- Displays **community notices** — local events, council information, public health messaging — for free
- Shows **local business advertising** at reduced rates, supporting the shops and services around the construction site
- Generates a **community fund** from a share of advertising revenue, managed by a local residents' group or parish council

The fund is transparent. Residents can see how much has been generated and how it's being spent. The developer gets credit for supporting the community. The construction company earns revenue from a structure that previously generated nothing. And the community gets something tangible from the disruption.

## Real Models to Draw From

This isn't entirely new. There are precedents:

**Community benefit from renewable energy:** In Scotland, community benefit funds linked to wind farm developments are well established. Developers contribute a fixed amount per megawatt of capacity to local communities, funding everything from village halls to broadband infrastructure.

**Social value in procurement:** The UK's Public Services (Social Value) Act 2012 requires public sector organisations to consider social value when awarding contracts. Construction firms that can demonstrate community investment through advertising-funded programmes score higher in procurement.

**BID levies:** Business Improvement Districts across the UK demonstrate that pooled, ring-fenced funding can transform local areas. The principle is the same: commercial activity generates a levy, and the levy funds improvements that benefit everyone.

## The Bigger Picture

The UK has a housing crisis. We need to build — a lot. The government's target of 1.5 million new homes requires construction on a massive scale, in communities across the country.

That construction is going to cause disruption. That's unavoidable. But the disruption doesn't have to be a one-way street.

If every major construction site in a city generated community benefit through advertising revenue, the cumulative effect would be transformative. Not as a replacement for proper infrastructure investment — but as an additional layer of value that didn't exist before.

The scaffolding is going up anyway. The hoardings are going up anyway. The question is whether we let that infrastructure sit there doing nothing — or whether we use it to give something back.

## A Challenge to the Industry

To developers: would you support a model where a share of construction-site advertising revenue went directly to the communities your projects affect?

To scaffold companies: would you partner with an advertising platform that built community benefit into its revenue model?

To councils: would you prioritise planning consent for digital construction-site media that included a community funding commitment?

The technology is ready. The revenue model works. The only question is whether the industry has the ambition to make construction sites work for everyone — not just the people building them.
