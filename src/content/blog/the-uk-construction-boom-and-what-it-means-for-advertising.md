The UK is building. From Liverpool's waterfront to Manchester's Northern Gateway, from Birmingham's Smithfield to London's Old Oak Common, cities across the country are undergoing transformation on a scale not seen in decades.

For the advertising industry, this construction boom isn't just background noise — it's creating a vast new canvas.

## The Scale of UK Construction

The numbers are striking. The UK construction industry is valued at over **£170 billion** annually, making it one of the largest sectors in the economy. In urban areas, the pace of development has accelerated:

- **Liverpool** — The £5.5bn Liverpool Waters scheme, Paddington Village, the Knowledge Quarter, and dozens of residential towers are reshaping the city centre
- **Manchester** — Over 15,000 residential units under construction across the city, with major schemes at Victoria North, Piccadilly, and MediaCity
- **Birmingham** — The HS2 arrival is driving a construction surge across Curzon Street, Smithfield, and the Jewellery Quarter
- **Leeds** — South Bank, the largest city-centre regeneration project in Europe, is transforming 253 acres of former industrial land
- **London** — Despite headwinds, the capital continues to see major programmes at Battersea, King's Cross, Stratford, and the Elizabeth Line corridor

Each of these projects means scaffolding. Hoardings. Cranes. And months — sometimes years — of construction activity in the most visible locations in each city.

## From Eyesore to Asset

Construction sites have traditionally been seen as a necessary inconvenience. Hoardings go up, pavements narrow, and pedestrians hurry past. The visual impact is, at best, neutral — and at worst, actively off-putting for neighbouring businesses and residents.

But what if construction boundaries weren't just functional — what if they were valuable?

A scaffold wrapped around a building on a city-centre high street occupies space that, in any other context, would command significant advertising revenue. A digital billboard in the same location might generate tens of thousands of pounds per month. Yet the scaffold itself — sitting in that exact spot for 12 months or more — generates nothing.

The disconnect is enormous.

## Why Cities Are Starting to Pay Attention

Local authorities across the UK are increasingly focused on the quality of construction boundaries. Several councils now mandate minimum standards for hoarding design, and some actively encourage decorative or community-focused wraps.

Digital screens on construction hoardings and scaffolding address multiple priorities simultaneously:

- **Revenue generation** — Councils can benefit from advertising revenue or levy agreements
- **Placemaking** — Screens can display community content, wayfinding, and public information alongside commercial advertising
- **Safety** — Well-lit digital hoardings improve pedestrian visibility and perceived safety around construction zones
- **Aesthetics** — A bright, well-maintained digital display is a significant improvement over faded vinyl or bare plywood

Cities like New York, Tokyo, and Dubai have already embraced construction-site media at scale. The UK is catching up — and the construction boom provides the perfect catalyst.

## The Liverpool Opportunity

Liverpool offers a particularly compelling case study. The city is experiencing its most significant period of construction since the post-war rebuilding, with over **£14 billion** in active or planned development projects.

Key factors that make Liverpool an ideal starting point:

- **Concentrated city centre** — High foot traffic in a compact area means maximum impressions per screen
- **Major regeneration zones** — Liverpool Waters, Paddington Village, and the Baltic Triangle offer clusters of construction activity
- **Strong local identity** — Liverpool's culture of bold visual communication (from the Superlambanana to the Tate Liverpool) makes it receptive to innovative media
- **Transport infrastructure** — The city's main stations, the waterfront, and arterial routes like the A57 and A580 provide high-traffic corridors past construction sites

For a platform launching scaffold-mounted digital advertising, Liverpool represents the ideal pilot market — concentrated enough to build a network, busy enough to attract advertisers, and growing fast enough to sustain it.

## What This Means for Advertisers

For brands, the UK construction boom creates a new class of DOOH inventory:

- **Hyper-local targeting** — Screens on specific construction sites can target specific postcodes, streets, and neighbourhoods
- **Contextual relevance** — Property developers, mortgage providers, interior brands, and local businesses can advertise directly adjacent to the homes and workplaces being built
- **Event-driven opportunities** — Construction near stadiums, arenas, and event venues creates temporary premium inventory during match days and concerts
- **Long-term presence** — Unlike most DOOH, construction-site screens offer months of continuous availability in one location

The construction boom isn't just building homes and offices. It's building a new media channel.
