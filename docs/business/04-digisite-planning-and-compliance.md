## 1. OVERVIEW — WHY PLANNING IS BUSINESS-CRITICAL FOR DIGISITE

Planning and compliance is not a back-office function for DigiSite — it is a core operational constraint that determines which sites can be activated, on what timeline, and under what conditions. Every single DigiSite installation requires express advertisement consent from the local planning authority before a single screen can go live. Failure to obtain this is a **criminal offence** under UK law.

This means planning must be treated as a **lead item** in the site mobilisation process — initiated as early as possible, with realistic timelines built into every site agreement and investor projection.

The good news: DigiSite's model is designed to work *with* the planning system, not around it. The temporary nature of construction sites, the community benefit angle, and the precedent of existing scaffold advertising all support approvable applications when prepared correctly.

## 2. LEGAL FRAMEWORK

### Primary Legislation
- **Town and Country Planning Act 1990 (as amended)** — overarching framework
- **Town and Country Planning (Control of Advertisements) (England) Regulations 2007** — the specific regulations governing all outdoor advertising consent in England
- **Planning (Listed Buildings and Conservation Areas) Act 1990** — additional controls for heritage assets

### The Key Rule
The display of advertisements is controlled through a specific approval process. It is a criminal offence to display an advertisement without consent. This applies to DigiSite's screens without exception — they are large, illuminated, and on scaffold structures, placing them firmly in the express consent category.

### Three Categories of Advertisement Control
1. **Exempt** — no consent required (small, non-illuminated signs, specific exempt classes). DigiSite screens do not fall into this category.
2. **Deemed Consent** — permitted without express application, provided specific conditions are met. DigiSite screens (large, illuminated, on scaffold) do not meet deemed consent conditions.
3. **Express Consent** — formal application to the local planning authority required. **This is DigiSite's category for every installation.**

## 3. ADVERTISEMENT CONSENT — THE APPLICATION PROCESS

### Who Decides
The Local Planning Authority (LPA) for the borough/district in which the site sits. In London, this is the relevant London Borough. In Liverpool, this is Liverpool City Council. In Manchester, this would be Manchester City Council, Salford City Council, etc. depending on which side of the boundary the site falls.

### Assessment Criteria
When deciding applications for advertisement consent, councils will consider factors like amenity, public safety and whether the advert is intrusive or distracting. They aim to strike a balance between business needs and protecting the local environment.

The LPA can **only** refuse on two grounds:
1. **Amenity** — visual impact on the street, effect on nearby buildings (especially historic), materials quality, scale, local character
2. **Public Safety** — driver visibility, distraction risk, pavement space, traffic sign obscuration, junction/crossing safety

Crucially, the council cannot refuse based on the advertising message content — only appearance and safety.

### Standard Conditions Applied to All Advertisements
Every consent will include the standard conditions from Schedule 2 of the 2007 Regulations:
- Maintained in a clean and tidy condition
- Maintained in a safe condition
- Permission of the site owner obtained
- Does not obscure or hinder road traffic signs
- Planning authority can require removal

### Application Fee
The costs for Consent to Display an Advertisement are: £168 for advertisements relating to the business on the premises, and £168 for advance signs directing the public to a business. However, DigiSite's installations are likely to fall outside these standard categories — the fee for specialised advertising structures (poster hoardings etc.) may differ. Budget approximately **£500–£800 per application** once consultant preparation time is included.

**Note:** Fees increased from 1 April 2025 and will rise annually with inflation (capped at 10% per year, measured by CPI from preceding September).

### Decision Timeline
Decisions on applications for advertisement consent should be made within 8 weeks from when the LPA accepts the application as valid. However, in practice:
- Complex or controversial applications regularly take 12–16 weeks
- Heritage-sensitive sites can take considerably longer
- Applications in London boroughs or major city centres often face longer queues
- The LPA may request additional information, restarting the clock

**Planning rule for DigiSite:** Always apply **minimum 12 weeks before intended go-live date.** For heritage sites, allow 20+ weeks. Factor this into every site agreement and partnership contract.

### Consent Duration
Once express consent is approved, you'll typically have 5 years until consent expires — you'll need to reapply to continue displaying the advert. For a construction site that will last 2–3 years, a single 5-year consent is sufficient for the full project duration.

### Appeals
If consent is refused, DigiSite has the right to appeal to the Planning Inspectorate within 8 weeks of the decision. The Planning Inspectorate handles these under the Commercial Appeals Service (CAS) and aims to fast-track them.

## 4. ILLUMINATION & BRIGHTNESS — THE CRITICAL TECHNICAL COMPLIANCE ISSUE

This is where DigiSite's 9,000 nit screens require careful management. UK planning regulations impose strict brightness limits on illuminated advertisements.

### Statutory Luminance Limits (Town and Country Planning (Control of Advertisements) Regulations 2007)
The permitted levels of luminance are: where the illuminated area is not more than 10 square metres, 600 candela per square metre; and where the illuminated area is more than 10 square metres, 300 candela per square metre.

DigiSite's standard 36-cabinet screen is 33.18 sq.m — well over 10m². The statutory permitted luminance is therefore **300 cd/m²** at night.

### The Apparent Conflict — and How to Resolve It
The Hikvision DS-D4257GO-2AAA operates at 9,000 nits (= 9,000 cd/m²) at factory preset. The statutory nighttime limit is 300 cd/m². This is NOT a fundamental obstacle — it is managed through:

1. **Auto-dimming photocells** — ambient light sensors automatically reduce brightness as daylight fades. At night the screen dims to comply with the 300 cd/m² limit. In full daylight, 9,000 nits is needed to be visible. This is standard industry practice and is a **planning condition that will be attached to every consent**.

2. **The Institute of Lighting Professionals (ILP) PLG05 Guide** — "The Brightness of Illuminated Advertisements Including Digital Displays" is the industry reference document that planning authorities use. It acknowledges that daytime ambient light levels are considerably higher than night, and provides a framework for variable brightness that planning inspectors accept.

3. **General limits** (from signage industry guidance): 600 candela per square metre for signs directly facing roads and 1,000 candela per square metre for all other locations — these are daytime operational guidance figures, not the statutory maxima.

### Practical Compliance Requirements for Every DigiSite Screen
- Auto-dimming photocell sensor — mandatory, fits to the screen controller
- Scheduled operating hours — typically off between 11pm–6am in residential areas (planning condition)
- Content change interval — minimum 5–10 seconds between static image changes; no rapid flashing or strobing
- No full-motion video outside designated zones (Piccadilly Circus-style areas)
- Maximum nighttime brightness: 300 cd/m² (built into CMS scheduling)
- Daytime brightness: up to 9,000 nits permitted — essential for visibility

## 5. SCAFFOLD ADVERTISING — SPECIFIC PLANNING POSITION

### Government Guidance (GOV.UK)
Buildings which are being renovated or are undergoing major structural work and which have scaffolding or netting around them may be considered suitable as temporary sites for shroud advertisements or large 'wrap' advertisements covering the face of the building. In all cases, express consent from the local planning authority will be required for these advertisements.

This confirms that scaffold advertising is explicitly recognised in planning guidance as a legitimate use case — DigiSite is not trying to establish a new principle; it is the *digital* version of an already-accepted practice.

### Key Difference: Digital vs Static
The planning system is familiar with static scaffold wraps and shrouds. DigiSite's digital screens are less common and will face additional scrutiny on:
- Luminance and nighttime impact
- Content change frequency (no video/rapid animation)
- Structural attachment to scaffold (must not compromise scaffold integrity)
- Duration and temporary nature

### Helpful Precedents to Reference in Applications
- Oxford Street digital scaffold screen (London, 2024) — first digital screen on Oxford Street, approved after thorough planning process. Sets precedent for commercial areas.
- Doka "SiteLight" — European precedent for LED screens on scaffold towers
- Existing scaffold wrap/shroud consents in the relevant LPA area — demonstrate acceptance of scaffold advertising in principle

## 6. HERITAGE — CONSERVATION AREAS & LISTED BUILDINGS

### When Heritage Applies
Heritage considerations arise when a DigiSite installation is:
- On a **listed building** (any grade: I, II*, or II)
- On a building **within a conservation area**
- On a building **adjacent to or in the setting of** a listed building or conservation area
- In a **World Heritage Site** buffer zone (e.g. parts of central London, Bath, Canterbury)

Heritage constraints are extremely common in UK city centres — many of DigiSite's most commercially attractive sites (busy urban locations with high footfall) will have some heritage dimension.

### Listed Buildings — Additional Consent Required
If the building to which the scaffolding is connected is included on the Statutory List of Buildings of Special Architectural or Historical Interest, listed building consent is likely to be required — in addition to advertisement consent. This is a separate application, separate fee, and assessed by different criteria (heritage impact rather than just amenity/safety).

Listed building grades:
- **Grade I** — exceptional interest (under 3% of listings). Digital screens very unlikely to be approved.
- **Grade II*** — particularly important (around 5%). Very difficult. Strong heritage statement essential.
- **Grade II** — special interest (over 90% of all listings). Applications possible with good heritage statement and sympathetic design. Most common scenario.

### Conservation Areas
The LPA will need to assess how much harm the proposed works will cause to the significance of the heritage asset, whether alternatives or mitigations could avoid or reduce it, and whether the harm is sufficiently justified.

For DigiSite in conservation areas:
- A **Heritage Statement** is required as part of the advertisement consent application
- The temporary nature of the screen (tied to construction duration) is a strong mitigating argument
- Community benefit arguments (improved streetscape vs unsightly scaffold) support the case
- Local authority conservation officers should be engaged informally before the formal application

### What a Heritage Statement Must Cover
A Heritage Statement is required for all applications affecting listed buildings, unlisted buildings in conservation areas or non-designated heritage assets, including applications affecting the setting for any of the above.

Structure of a Heritage Statement for DigiSite:
1. **Description of the heritage asset and its setting** — age, architectural character, significance, contribution to conservation area
2. **Assessment of significance** — what makes it important, what its vulnerabilities are
3. **Description of the proposed DigiSite installation** — screen dimensions, mounting method, duration, operating hours, brightness controls
4. **Impact assessment** — how the screen affects the significance of the asset during the construction period
5. **Mitigation measures** — brightness controls, operating hours, temporary nature, reversibility
6. **Public benefit argument** — improved appearance vs scaffold, community information content, economic regeneration contribution

### Heritage Statement: DigiSite's Key Arguments
- **Temporariness** — the screen is only present during the construction phase; once scaffold is struck, the heritage asset is fully restored
- **Reversibility** — no permanent fixing to the heritage fabric; all attachment is to the scaffold, not the building
- **Improvement over alternative** — a digital screen displaying curated content is visually superior to blank scaffold netting or unsightly wraps
- **Precedent** — static scaffold wraps have been approved on heritage buildings; digital screens are a more controllable, better-managed version

### Practical Strategy
- **Tier sites by heritage sensitivity** at the outset of every site assessment
- **Tier 1 (No heritage):** Standard advertisement consent only — fastest route
- **Tier 2 (Conservation area, no listed building):** Advertisement consent + Heritage Statement — allow extra 4–6 weeks
- **Tier 3 (Adjacent to or on Grade II listed):** Advertisement consent + Listed building consent + Heritage Statement — allow 16–24 weeks, instruct specialist heritage consultant
- **Tier 4 (Grade I or II* listed building):** Assess very carefully; likely to be unviable or require exceptional justification
- Engage conservation officers informally (pre-application advice meeting) for Tier 2 and above — this is free or low-cost and dramatically improves approval prospects

## 7. APPLICATION PACKAGE — WHAT TO SUBMIT

Every advertisement consent application for DigiSite should include:

### Standard Documents (all applications)
- Completed advertisement consent application form (via Planning Portal or direct to LPA)
- **Site location plan** at 1:1250 or 1:2500 scale
- **Block plan** showing site context at 1:500
- **Existing photographs** of the building/scaffold from multiple angles
- **Proposed elevations** showing the screen in situ — dimensioned, to scale, showing position on scaffold
- **Screen specification sheet** — Hikvision DS-D4257GO-2AAA datasheet, confirming IP66, brightness controls, auto-dimming
- **Design and Access Statement** — explaining the design rationale, materials, fixings, removal process
- **Operational parameters** — operating hours, maximum brightness at night (300 cd/m²), content change frequency, kill switch provision
- **Structural note** — confirming screen attachment is to scaffold only, not to building fabric; noting that scaffold contractor has reviewed the additional load

### Additional Documents for Heritage Sites
- **Heritage Statement** (see section 6 above)
- **Conservation Area Appraisal reference** — confirm you've read and addressed the relevant LPA conservation area guidance
- **Historic England consultation** (for Grade I or II* sites — informal consultation before application)

### Pre-Application Engagement (Recommended for All Sites)
Before submitting formally, DigiSite should:
- Contact the LPA's planning/advertising team informally to flag the proposal
- Request a pre-application advice meeting for any complex or heritage-sensitive site
- Ask specifically whether the LPA has any adopted local advertising policies or supplementary planning documents that apply
- Note any local article 4 directions that may restrict deemed consents (and therefore indicate a stricter local approach)

## 8. COMMON PLANNING CONDITIONS — WHAT TO EXPECT

Every approved DigiSite consent will include conditions. Typical conditions for digital scaffold advertising:

| Condition | Typical Requirement |
|---|---|
| Operating hours | Off between 11pm–6am (may vary by location; residential areas stricter) |
| Maximum nighttime luminance | ≤ 300 cd/m² after dusk (photocell controlled) |
| Content change interval | Minimum 5–10 seconds between changes; no rapid animation |
| No full-motion video | Static images or slow transitions only (outside special zones) |
| Duration | Tied to duration of scaffold/construction works |
| Removal | Screen and all fixings removed within [X] days of scaffold striking |
| Maintenance | Screen maintained in clean and safe condition at all times |
| Structural certification | Copy of structural engineer's approval to be held on site |
| Emergency kill switch | Ability to turn off immediately if required |

**These conditions should be built into DigiSite's standard operating procedures from day one** — they are not obstacles, they are the framework within which the business operates.

## 9. HEALTH & SAFETY COMPLIANCE

### CDM Regulations 2015 (Construction Design and Management)
Installing screens on scaffolding constitutes construction work and triggers CDM obligations:
- **Principal Contractor** (the scaffold/construction contractor) has overall site CDM responsibility
- DigiSite acts as a **sub-contractor** and must comply with the principal contractor's Construction Phase Plan
- DigiSite must provide its own **method statement** and **risk assessment** for each screen installation
- All DigiSite operatives must have valid **CSCS cards** and complete the principal contractor's site induction before working on site

### Working at Height Regulations 2005
- All work at height must be planned, supervised, and carried out by competent persons
- Risk assessment required per installation
- Collective protection (scaffold handrails, toe boards) preferred over personal protection (harnesses) where possible
- Full harness + fall arrest required where collective protection is impractical
- Equipment inspections documented before use

### Method Statement Contents (per installation)
Each DigiSite installation must have a written method statement covering:
- Sequence of operations
- Equipment to be used (screens, lifting, tools)
- Exclusion zones during installation
- PPE requirements
- Emergency procedures
- Named competent persons

### Insurance Requirements
- **Public Liability Insurance** — minimum £5 million (many construction sites require £10 million)
- **Employers' Liability Insurance** — legal requirement as soon as any employees or labour-only subcontractors engaged
- **Equipment/Asset Insurance** — screens and hardware (high value — insurers will require secure storage and asset tracking)
- **Professional Indemnity** — if DigiSite provides design/engineering services
- All subcontractors must hold their own public liability — check certificates before engaging

## 10. BUSINESS RATES

Advertising hoardings and digital displays in the UK are subject to business rates (non-domestic property rating). Each DigiSite installation may be assessed for business rates as a rateable hereditament.

Key points:
- The Valuation Office Agency (VOA) assesses rateable value
- Temporary installations (tied to construction duration) may be treated differently to permanent screens
- The site owner/developer may already be assessed for the site — DigiSite's installation could be treated as part of that assessment
- Budget for potential business rates liability per site in the financial model
- Engage a specialist rating surveyor once pilot sites are confirmed — retrospective rates bills are common in the OOH industry

## 11. PLANNING CONSULTANT — BUILDING THE CAPABILITY

### In-House vs Outsourced
For the pilot phase, DigiSite should use a **specialist planning consultant** on a per-application basis. Look for consultants with:
- Specific experience in advertisement consent applications
- Familiarity with DOOH/digital signage applications
- Knowledge of heritage and conservation area applications
- Strong relationships with LPAs in DigiSite's target cities (Liverpool, Manchester, Salford, Leeds)

### Building In-House Knowledge
As DigiSite scales, pattern recognition is key — the same arguments, same document templates, same application structure work across most sites with local variations. After the first 5–10 applications, DigiSite should have a **standard application template pack** that can be adapted per site, reducing consultant costs over time.

### Key Consultant Contacts to Find (Liverpool/Merseyside and North West)
- Planning consultants specialising in advertisement consent in Liverpool/Merseyside and Greater Manchester
- Conservation/heritage consultants familiar with Liverpool, Manchester, Salford conservation areas (Liverpool in particular has significant heritage designations — historic waterfront, Georgian Quarter, multiple listed structures)
- Rating surveyors familiar with DOOH/OOH advertising hereditaments
